Annuities & Loans

  1. Suppose Anita wants to save up for a down payment on a home. She opens an annuity account with her bank that offers 3% annual interest compounded monthly, and she plans to deposit $800 per month.
    1. How much will she have saved for a down payment after five years?
    2. How much would she had saved if she instead stashed $800 into a coffee can burried in her backyard each month?
  2. Over the course of his life, Billiam has saved up $1.2 million in a retirement account that yields 2.5% annual interest. He’s 67 now, and ready to retire.
    1. If Billiam anticipates living to be 100-years-old and wants his saving to last that long, how much can he afford to withdraw each month?
    2. Billiam decides that’s not enough money per month to support his extravagant lifestyle, so he withdraws $7000 per month in his retirement instead. How old will Billiam be when he runs out of money?
    3. Billiam finds this unacceptable! He is convinced he’ll live to be 100-years-old, and he will settle for no less than $7000 per month in his retirement. The only option then is to defer his retirement and keep working. If Billiam can contribute $2,200 per month into his retirement account while he’s working, when can he finally retire?
  3. Suppose you are contributing $600 a month into a savings annuity account, intended to be your retirement account, that pays an annual interest rate of 3.7% compounded monthly.

  4. Suppose you bought a house. After making a down-payment, you apply for a mortgage with an annual 5.99% interest rate on the remaining balance of $251,200. Recall that the formula relating these values to your monthly mortgage payments \(P\) is \[S = P\left(\frac{1-\left(1+\frac{r}{12}\right)^{-12t}}{\frac{r}{12}}\right)\,.\]
    1. Before plugging in numbers, solve this equation for \(P\) so you have a formula for your monthly mortgage payments. (Think before you start—this can be done in one step.)
    2. Using this formula, calculate what your monthly payments would be if you got a 15-year mortgage versus a 30-year mortgage.
    3. Because the balance of the mortgage accrues interest while you are paying it off, the total amount you pay will be more than the initial mortgage amount; you are paying for the interest too. Using your answers to the previous question, calculate how much money you’d be paying total in the case of a 15-year mortgage and the case of a 30-year mortgage. (Hint: don’t overthink these calculations.)
    4. Now using the Mortgage worksheet of the Excel spreadsheet, check your calculations from the previous questions.

Your Own Financials

  1. While you’ve got the Mortgage worksheet open, you might as well play with numbers to get a sense of what your personal situation will be when/if you decide to buy a home. There’s two critical questions to answer:

    • Where do you plan on buying a home (living)?
    • What sort of income do you anticipate having?

    Look up average home prices in the area you’re planning on living, but also look up lower-end home prices (if there are enough luxury homes in the area, they’ll skew the average, making it misleading). Then try to estimate your future annual income: if you have a career/title in mind, look up starting salaries/earnings for that career.

    Some quick facts: First, the vast majority of mortgages have a 30-year term, so use \(N=30\) unless you have a good reason otherwise. Next, the typical interest rate of a mortgage is set by the Federal Funds Rate. Look up what this currently is for your mortgage rate \(r\), and look up any news on speculation for how it will change in the future. Last, the classic advice is that “28% of your gross income should go towards housing.” This advice is suspect given recent trends in the housing market and labor market, but should still be considered when deciding what monthly mortgage payment \(P\) you can afford given your projected income.

  2. Notice that there’s Student Loans worksheet in this Excel file too. If you have them, you could take this time to work out the details of your own student loans (You don’t have to write anything on this page!) What is the interest rate on your loans? How much are you taking out in loans each year/semester? There’s one initial important thing to know: are your loans subsidized or unsubsidized?

    • Subsidized students loans don’t accrue interest while you are still attending school and during some “grace period” after graduation until (ideally) you find employment, because the government is paying the interest.
    • Unsubsidized loans simply accrue interest.
    While you are not making payments, if your loans are accruing interest the balance of your student debt can be calculated using the formula for compound interest. As soon as you begin making payments, interest is still accruing, so the balance will be described the annuity formula we’ve been discussing.
