Fitting Lines to Data Points

The table below gives the projected life expectancy (life span) of a person born in the United States for select birth years from 1920 and through 2018 according to the CDC.

  1. First, fill in the empty row of the table labelled Years since 1900 with the number of years since 1900. E.g. the year 1960 is 60 years since 1900. We will consider this row of data our independent variable \(x.\)

    It’s almost always a good idea to offset the data we’re using as our independent variable like this to (1) make computations simpler, and (2) to make the labels on any figures we product less cluttered and easier to read.
  2. Using technology — either your TI graphing calculator, or Desmos, or Microsoft Excel — plot a scatter-plot of this data using Years since 1900 as the independent variable \(x\) and Life Span as the dependent variable \(y.\)
  3. Graphs of these two lines on the plot containing the scatter-plot.

  4. Discuss as a group which of these two lines appears to “fit the data best”? Note, this is subjective. Can you come up with a line that appears to fit the data better than either of these two? The variables/sliders feature of Desmos can be helpful here.

    At the end of class we’ll discuss linear regression and the line of best fit. Be sure to compare the line you came up with by eye-balling it to this line.
